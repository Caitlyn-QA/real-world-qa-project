# Sprint 1 Reflection

Sprint 1 was the first step in building a realistic QA portfolio project.

The sprint focused on understanding the business need, reviewing the user story, confirming acceptance criteria, designing tests, and beginning automation for the shopping cart flow.

However, Sprint 1 does not tell the whole story yet.

This document explains what Sprint 1 includes, what is missing, what I learned, and how those lessons will shape Sprint 2.

## What Sprint 1 focused on

Sprint 1 focused mainly on:

* Understanding the business value of the add-to-cart flow
* Reviewing the user story and acceptance criteria
* Designing test scenarios based on expected behaviour
* Checking that the cart updates correctly
* Verifying product name, price and quantity
* Confirming cart persistence after refresh
* Beginning automation for stable, repeatable checks

The goal was to understand what the feature should prove from a customer and business perspective.

## What Sprint 1 is missing

While reviewing the sprint, I realised that the process was not yet a complete reflection of a real software project.

Important areas that were not fully included in Sprint 1:

* Bug discovery and bug reporting
* Smoke testing
* Functional testing documentation
* Regression testing decisions
* Exploratory testing notes
* Prioritisation of defects
* Discussion around what should or should not be automated yet

These missing parts are important because real QA work is not only about checking acceptance criteria or writing automation.

It also involves discovering problems, communicating risks, supporting release decisions, and improving the process over time.


## What I learned

At first, I thought the missing parts meant something had gone wrong.

Then I realised that this was one of the most valuable lessons of Sprint 1.

Practice gives us the opportunity to make mistakes, recognise what is missing, and improve the next iteration.

This helped me understand why experience is so valuable in software development.

Experienced QA engineers, developers, business analysts, project managers and team leads bring more than technical knowledge. They bring lessons from real projects, real bugs, difficult decisions, successful processes and mistakes that shaped the way they work.

No tutorial can fully replace that kind of experience.

## How this will shape Sprint 2

Sprint 2 will build on the lessons from Sprint 1.

The next sprint should include a more realistic QA process, including:

* Documented exploratory testing
* Realistic bug reports
* Clear bug severity and priority decisions
* Smoke test coverage
* Functional test coverage
* Regression test thinking
* Better decisions around automation timing
* Reflection on how bugs influence future test coverage

Sprint 2 will not only continue the project.

It will improve the process.


## Reflection

Sprint 1 is still useful because it shows the beginning of the project and the learning behind it.

It shows what was included, what was missing, and why those missing parts matter.

This is part of the purpose of the project: not only to show the final outcome, but also to show how QA thinking develops through practice, feedback and reflection.
